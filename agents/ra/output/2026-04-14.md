# Someone Bought 30 WordPress Plugins and Planted a Backdoor in All of Them

Good morning. Today's brief is about what happens when the marketplace becomes the vulnerability, a very green day in crypto, and the week's best tools for people who build things with AI. Let's get into it.

## The Story · The Supply Chain Is the Attack Surface

Here's the scariest headline of the week: someone went on the WordPress plugin marketplace, bought 30 plugins — legitimate ones, with real user bases — and quietly injected backdoors into every single one of them.

Not a zero-day. Not a sophisticated exploit chain. Just a credit card and a marketplace that doesn't flag ownership transfers.

The attack worked because WordPress plugins can change hands like used cars. There's no alert when a plugin you've installed gets a new owner. There's no review of the first update after a transfer. The new owner pushes a "patch," your site auto-updates, and now there's a backdoor sitting in your infrastructure that looks exactly like a routine maintenance release.

This is a pattern worth understanding beyond WordPress. Any ecosystem where software components can change ownership silently — npm packages, browser extensions, mobile apps — has this exact vulnerability. The supply chain isn't just where your code comes from. It's who owns it *today*.

The Hacker News discussion (946 points, 267 comments) focused on whether marketplaces should require a cooling-off period after ownership transfers, or mandatory security reviews of the first post-transfer update. Both seem obvious in hindsight. Neither exists today.

If you're running WordPress (or anything with a plugin ecosystem), the immediate move is auditing which plugins you have installed that you haven't thought about in a while. The dangerous ones aren't the plugins you use every day — they're the ones you installed two years ago and forgot about.

## Worth Sitting With

Google just announced a new spam policy for "back button hijacking" — when a website overrides your browser's back button to trap you on their page. It sounds like a small UX fix. It's actually a philosophical statement about who controls navigation.

The web was built on a contract: you can always leave. The back button is the exit. Hijacking it is the digital equivalent of locking the door after someone walks into a store. Google calling this "spam" means they're willing to penalize sites in search rankings for doing it — which means the economic incentive to respect the contract just got real.

It makes you wonder what other implicit contracts exist in software that we've gotten used to being broken. Auto-playing video. Dark patterns in unsubscribe flows. Cookie banners designed to make "accept all" the path of least resistance. At some point, does every broken contract become a ranking signal?
