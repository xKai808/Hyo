## The Hardware Man Takes the Wheel

Good morning. Apple just made its first CEO change in fifteen years — and the choice is a signal worth reading carefully. Alongside that: the EU is forcing batteries back into your phone, Alibaba dropped another competitive model, and two AI research papers landed today that quietly challenge something most of us assumed we understood. ## The Story · Tim Cook Hands the Keys to the Engineer

After fifteen years, Tim Cook is stepping back. He'll become Apple's Executive Chairman — still close, not gone. The new CEO is John Ternus, who ran Apple's Hardware Engineering division and was the chief architect of the M-chip transition that moved the Mac off Intel and onto Apple Silicon.

This is not a crisis. It's a planned succession, the kind Cook has been quietly building toward for years. But the choice of Ternus over an operations or finance executive is worth sitting with for a minute.

Cook's Apple was a particular kind of machine. He took the products Jobs left behind, made them manufacturable at planetary scale, and built the services layer — the App Store, Apple TV+, iCloud, Apple Pay — into a revenue stream that now rivals the iPhone itself in its strategic importance. The company Cook ran was defined less by what it invented than by how well it executed. That is not a knock. Supply chain mastery and margin discipline are genuine strategic gifts, and Cook deployed both at a level almost no CEO in history has matched.

Ternus is a different instrument. His signature achievement is the M-chip transition — the decision to abandon Intel and build Apple's own silicon, a bet that looked audacious in 2020 and looks prescient now. Apple Silicon has a genuine lead in performance-per-watt, and in an era when on-device AI is becoming the next frontier, that lead has compounding value. Every model that runs locally on an iPhone rather than in a data center somewhere is a privacy feature, a latency feature, and a competitive moat all at once. Ternus understands that. He built the thing.

Two reads circulate this morning.

The optimistic one: Apple is doubling down on hardware at precisely the moment hardware matters most again. Custom silicon — Apple's M-chips, Google's TPUs, Amazon's Graviton — has become the new strategic moat. A CEO whose mental model is "what can this chip do" may push harder on on-device inference, on Vision Pro's second act, on the kind of deeply integrated hardware-software experiences that have always been Apple's differentiator and that cloud-dependent competitors genuinely cannot replicate.

The cautious one: Ternus has never run a P&L at this scale. Apple's regulatory fights in Europe, its content business, its relationship with developers who increasingly feel squeezed by App Store economics — these are not hardware problems. The learning curve is real, and the company is too large to absorb a slow start gracefully.

What's worth watching: Cook spent his first few years being underestimated by people who thought he was just a caretaker. He wasn't. The question for Ternus is whether WWDC and the next product cycle reveal a CEO who sees a new mountain to climb — or one who is managing the altitude already gained.

## Also Moving · Rules, Models, and Infrastructure

**The EU's battery mandate is real and it lands in 2027.** Every phone and tablet sold in Europe will need a user-replaceable battery. This is the regulation the right-to-repair movement has wanted for a decade and that Apple has resisted through every design iteration since the sealed aluminum unibody. The question is less "will Apple comply" — they will — and more whether a genuinely replaceable iPhone ends up feeling like an iPhone. Early engineering analysis suggests it's possible without abandoning water resistance or the chassis profile. We'll see what the tradeoffs look like when the designs land.

On the same day, Brussels launched an age-verification app for adults-only content access, and hackers reported bypassing it in two minutes. The mechanism was straightforward: the app trusted client-side assertions that a third-party check had been completed, without server-side validation. No zero-day (an unpatched vulnerability the vendor doesn't know about) required. It's a good reminder that regulatory ambition and implementation quality are different muscles entirely.

**Qwen3.6-Max-Preview is out from Alibaba.** No full benchmarks yet, but the early reception on Hacker News was strong enough to rank it second today. The story here isn't one model — it's cadence. Alibaba has been shipping meaningful frontier-tier updates at a pace that keeps real pressure on the claim that Chinese open models lag U.S. closed ones. That gap was real eighteen months ago. The distance has been shrinking in public view.

**Vercel had an outage last week** triggered by a Roblox cheat tool and an AI scraper hitting the platform simultaneously and cascading function invocations past capacity limits. The mechanism is specific to serverless architecture (functions that scale automatically can also be overwhelmed automatically), and the recovery reportedly took several hours. No platform is immune to this class of attack. What matters is how quickly the post-mortem informs guardrails.

## The Lab · What AI Can Newly Do

**LACE: reasoning paths that learn from each other mid-run**

Standard practice when running LLM reasoning today: sample multiple parallel chains, pick the best output, discard the rest. The problem is that those chains are fully isolated — they can't tell each other what they've found. LACE (Lattice Attention for Cross-thread Exploration) repurposes the model's own attention mechanism to enable cross-thread communication while the chains are still running. Paths exploring different corners of the problem can share intermediate findings in real time.

The insight is subtle but important. When you sample five independent chains, you might think you're getting diversity. But if all five draw on the same internal representations, they'll fail in the same direction — just with slightly different phrasing. LACE is trying to produce genuine epistemic diversity, not the illusion of it.

Reach for this when: the solution space is large and non-obvious — complex debugging, multi-step planning, research synthesis where the answer isn't in one place. Less useful for narrow, well-defined tasks where one clean chain is sufficient.

**LLM Reasoning is Latent, Not the Chain of Thought**

A position paper dropped today with a claim that has been building under the surface of the field for a while: the chain-of-thought text a model produces is not a faithful transcript of its reasoning. The actual computation happens in latent space — the internal vector representations flowing through the model's layers — and the text is a kind of narration produced afterward, not a window into the process.

This matters because the entire framework around reasoning benchmarks, interpretability, and inference-time interventions assumes CoT is the thing. If it isn't — if the real reasoning is happening somewhere the text doesn't capture — then benchmarks that reward polished CoT may be measuring writing quality more than reasoning quality, and interventions that modify the written chain may be adjusting the wrong layer entirely.

This one belongs in the mental model before building any reasoning-dependent pipeline. Not a "stop everything" finding, but a "hold this assumption lightly" one.

## Worth Sitting With

There's a thesis implicit in the Ternus appointment that doesn't get stated directly: hardware is back as the strategic layer that matters most.

For most of the last decade, the conventional wisdom in tech ran the other direction. Software ate the world; software was where margins lived; hardware was a commodity you sourced as cheaply as possible. The ideal tech CEO was a product thinker or a business model thinker, not someone who got excited about die shrinks.

What's changed is inference. Running AI models at scale is expensive, and the companies that built their own silicon — Apple with M-chips, Google with TPUs, Amazon with Graviton — have a cost and capability advantage that software cannot paper over. The inference layer is becoming the new platform layer, and the inference layer is made of chips.

If you accept that framing, putting a hardware engineer in the CEO chair at the most valuable hardware-software company in the world in 2026 looks less like a sentimental nod to Apple's product roots and more like a calculated repositioning for the next decade. The era when software people should naturally run hardware companies may be ending — not because software doesn't matter, but because the bottleneck has moved.

Question worth sitting with: if the inference layer becomes the platform, which company is actually best positioned to own it — the one with the best models, or the one with the best chips?
