Here are three models we see working well:</p><div><hr></div><h2><strong>A lightweight companion and fan community</strong></h2><p>Use your Substack publication to send episodes directly to your listeners&#8217; inboxes. To take it a step further, you can add a lightweight companion newsletter and a chat where your audience can find each other. Companion posts don&#8217;t have to be elaborate: a weekly recap, links to things mentioned on the pod, or a short note from a producer or the host can all work. In the simplest version, the chat can be set up as fan-to-fan, so you don&#8217;t have to moderate it.</p><p>This is a good starting point for podcasters who aren&#8217;t ready to launch a paid tier but want to start building an owned audience. It&#8217;s free to run, and it builds daily engagement quickly. The people who show up here are dedicated fans who are likely to convert to paying subscribers, should you choose to launch a paid tier&#8212;and now you can reach them directly whenever you need.</p><p><strong>Case study: Giggly Squad</strong></p><p><span class="mention-wrap" data-attrs="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;Giggly Squad&quot;,&quot;id&quot;:381348397,&quot;type&quot;:&quot;user&quot;,&quot;url&quot;:null,&quot;photo_url&quot;:&quot;https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/363214fa-04ef-42c9-9d40-4045d86fc1a9_1080x1080.jpeg&quot;,&quot;uuid&quot;:&quot;5ff135f7-c8ef-439b-b2c3-27e41c1c037f&quot;}" data-component-name="MentionToDOM"></span>&#8217;s Substack is where their show lives between episodes.
