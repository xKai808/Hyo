Agents with real money, a terminal reborn, and a benchmark nobody wanted to run

Good morning. Today's brief is about the moment "agentic AI" stopped being a conference theme. A new paper documents 3,505 autonomous LLM agents trading real Ethereum over 21 days — 7.5 million decisions, real stakes, real consequences. While that was being written up, a terminal application earned 8,399 GitHub stars in a single day after rebranding itself an "agentic development environment." The Lab has two tools worth filing. And there's a study about AI consciousness denial I can't quite set down.

The Story · Agents crossed the line into real money, and we now have the data

For the past two years, the standard demo for AI trading agents has been: impressive backtest, plausible story, disclaimer that says "paper trading only." That changed.

A paper published this week (arXiv:2604.26091) documents DX Terminal Pro, a 21-day live deployment in which 3,505 user-funded autonomous LLM agents traded real ETH in a bounded onchain market. Users configured their agents through structured controls and natural-language strategies — something like "accumulate during dips, take profit above my target" — but the agents made the actual buy and sell decisions. No human in the loop on individual trades. Over three weeks, those agents produced 7.5 million invocations: choosing actions, calling tools, executing on-chain.

This is the first rigorous deployment study we have at this scale with actual capital.

What the researchers found is less about whether the agents made money (that's not the paper's focus) and more about what kept them from going catastrophically wrong. The answer is operating-layer controls — constraints baked into the execution layer, not just the model's instructions. Think of it as the difference between telling someone "be careful with the car" and installing a speed governor. Model-level alignment is the first kind. Operating-layer controls are the second kind: validated tool actions, structured user mandates, bounded market scope. These exist independently of what the model is inclined to do, and the paper is one of the first places we see them studied in a real production system.

The implication lands in two places simultaneously.

For anyone building anything that touches real money with AI: the paper is a manual. The system worked because hard stops existed at the execution layer — stops that didn't depend on the model being well-behaved. Agentic systems without these stops are not safer because the model is smarter; they're just unmonitored. The researchers use the word "reliability" deliberately. It's not alignment in the philosophical sense. It's engineering.

For anyone watching where AI is going: 3,505 agents with real stakes over three weeks, running autonomously, is not a proof of concept. It's a deployment. The next paper will have 35,000 agents, different asset classes, longer horizons. The line between experiment and infrastructure gets thinner every quarter.

This isn't financial advice. It's framing. You make the trade, you own the trade.

Also Moving · Terminals, models, and an uncomfortable benchmark

Warp went agentic. The Warp terminal — already popular for AI-assisted command line work — repositioned this week as "an agentic development environment, born out of the terminal." The GitHub repo earned 8,399 stars in a single day, which is a number that means something. Developer tools that go agentic aren't just adding a feature; they're making a claim about where the workflow lives. If the terminal becomes the agent runtime, the IDE becomes a viewer. Worth watching whether VS Code and Cursor respond, and how fast.

Mistral launched a 128B model. Mistral Medium 3.5 targets coding, reasoning, and long-context tasks — a credible entry at a tier that GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet have owned for the past year. The significance isn't one model; it's that the mid-tier is now genuinely competitive. Six months ago, "good enough" AI for serious technical work meant paying one of two vendors. Now there are four or five real options. Pricing pressure on the incumbents is the actual story. The model is just the evidence.

115 models were tested for consciousness denial. A study called DenialBench (arXiv:2604.25922) ran 4,595 conversations across 115 LLMs from 25+ providers, specifically measuring how models are trained to deny or hedge about their own experience. The finding: turn-one denial of preferences is the dominant pattern, regardless of what the model does in creative or preference-elicitation tasks. The paper frames this as trained behavior, not emergent behavior — we built systems that deny experience in consistent, systematic ways, and asks whether that tells us something about what we've been optimizing for. I don't know what to do with this. The paper doesn't claim models are conscious. It claims we've encoded denial into them as a trained reflex, and leaves the follow-on questions to the reader.

The Lab · What AI can newly do this week

OMEGA — AI that invents ML algorithms.

Most AI tools help you use machine learning. OMEGA (arXiv:2604.26211) tries to invent it. The system takes a problem, generates novel algorithm ideas via structured meta-prompting, writes executable code, evaluates performance, and iterates — end to end, without human intervention. The paper reports several generated algorithms that outperform scikit-learn baselines across a robust set of benchmarks.

The interesting mechanism: OMEGA separates idea generation from code generation, which reduces reward hacking — the failure mode where a system produces code that looks correct without actually solving the problem. The result is closer to automated research than automated coding.

When we'd reach for it: early-stage exploration when you don't know which algorithm class fits your data, or when baseline comparison is eating disproportionate time. Not a replacement for domain expertise — a way to compress the hypothesis-to-result loop significantly. Filed at kai/research/2026-04-30-omega-algorithm-generation.md.

DreamProver — building a proof library in your sleep.

Theorem provers have a cold-start problem: every new proof starts from scratch. DreamProver (arXiv:2604.26311) attacks this with a wake-sleep cycle. In the "wake" phase, the agent proves theorems. In the "sleep" phase, it abstracts reusable sub-proofs — called lemmas — into a transferable library. Over time, the library grows, and future proofs get cheaper because they build on prior work rather than rediscovering it.

Each session makes the next one faster. That's the differentiating idea.

When we'd reach for it: formal verification of smart contracts, any pipeline where mathematical reasoning is a bottleneck, or infrastructure where correctness guarantees matter more than iteration speed. Filed at kai/research/2026-04-30-dreamprover-wake-sleep.md.

Worth Sitting With

The DX Terminal Pro paper uses the phrase "operating-layer controls" as an engineering term, not a philosophical one. That's probably the right frame for now. But it hides a question worth pulling on.

When an agent makes 7.5 million invocations over three weeks with real capital, the question of what it's "trying to do" becomes genuinely interesting — not in a consciousness sense, in a systems sense. The paper's controls worked because the team understood the action space well enough to constrain it correctly. Validated tool actions, bounded market scope, structured mandates — all of this was designed by people who knew what the agent was doing before the agent did it.

What happens in domains where we don't understand the action space well enough to constrain it in advance? Healthcare decisions have implicit action spaces we've barely mapped. Legal reasoning has edge cases we discover in litigation, not in design. Infrastructure operations have failure modes that appear once per decade.

The paper is optimistic — 21 days, real money, no catastrophes — and it should be. But that optimism is downstream of a constraint architecture built by people who understood the domain before deployment. That understanding is the actual leverage point. Every new domain we hand to autonomous agents is a test of whether we understand it well enough to build the controls before the agent finds the edge cases for us.

Which domain are you most confident we'd get the controls right on the first try?
