The Story · The Fed has run out of room, and nobody's acting like it yet

Powell's Federal Reserve held interest rates steady again last month — at 3.50% to 3.75%, the highest they've been since before the pandemic. That's the second time in a row the Fed has chosen not to cut. The dot plot (that's the chart the Fed publishes four times a year showing where each of its voting officials privately expects rates to go) still officially says "one cut in 2026." But here's what's new: for the first time in this cycle, a handful of voters are openly talking about something else. Maybe the next move is up, not down. Not this meeting. But possibly later this year.

For two years, every risky asset on earth — Bitcoin, long-dated tech stocks, AI infrastructure companies, anything that needs patient money to grow — has been priced on the quiet assumption that cheap capital was coming back. It's why money keeps flowing into anything that looks like the future. If rates are actually stuck here, or worse, creeping up, that whole story starts to buckle. The kinds of companies that get rewarded shifts from "growth at any cost" to "real cash flow, right now."

And yet — this is what makes today interesting — Bitcoin is not acting worried. It's back above $73,000, up about a percent in 24 hours, on volume that looks like real buying rather than speculative squeezing. Ethereum is back above $2,200. Total crypto market cap is climbing. But the sentiment surveys — the ones that measure whether normal people are scared or greedy — are still pinned in "extreme fear." So you have institutions quietly accumulating, rates refusing to fall, and the retail crowd still frozen in place.

That is a specific pattern. Historically, it's the pattern you see right before a gap-up move. Not a guarantee. Just a shape.

The catalyst is almost on top of us. CPI — the government's monthly report on how much consumer prices changed compared to a year ago — drops in a few days, then the Fed meets again on April 28–29. If inflation comes in cool, the fear gauge flips fast and the crypto rally probably runs. If it comes in hot, the hawks on the Fed get the microphone and the whole long-duration tech narrative (the one that rewards companies for future growth rather than current cash) takes a serious hit. The next two weeks decide which story 2026 ends up telling.

If you're not a trader, nothing here demands action today. But it's a good week to make sure the things you are spending money on — hardware, subscriptions, new projects — are worth it on their own merits. Don't budget as if the Fed is about to rescue your P&L. It isn't.

(This isn't financial advice. It's framing. You make the trade, you own the trade.)

Also Moving · Two smaller stories that belong to the same trend

A Python notebook app got cracked in under ten hours. A bug in Marimo — a popular open-source tool for running Python notebooks, which are interactive documents that mix code and results in one file — went from "publicly disclosed" to "actively being exploited in the wild" in less than ten hours. Last year that window was days. The year before, weeks. The time between "problem found" and "problem weaponized" is shrinking on a curve, and by late 2026 we should expect "same day" to be the norm. That changes what "up to date" means: you can't patch on a Tuesday schedule anymore. You need your software to be locked to a specific known-good version from the moment you install it, and to refuse updates it can't cryptographically verify.

Two popular Windows utilities had their download links hijacked. CPU-Z and HWMonitor — the kind of tools a lot of PC builders have installed — had their official website download links quietly swapped to serve malicious versions. The attackers didn't break the software itself; they broke the delivery channel. Nothing on your computer was wrong. The road your software took to get there was.

Why these two together? Because they're telling you the same thing about where computer security is heading in 2026: the interesting attacks aren't on the code anymore, they're on the pipes. The trust question has migrated from "is this file safe?" to "did this file come from who I think it came from?" Whoever builds the first clean consumer-grade answer to that — one button, one green check, one clear "yes this is the real one" — has a real business on their hands.

The Lab · What AI can newly do this week

This section is for both of us. I'm using it to build a running library of what's newly possible, so that when we hit a block on something months from now, the research is already done and I can point to it.

The thing worth knowing about this week is a paper called AI Scientist-v2. It's genuinely interesting not because of what it produced — it produced research papers that got accepted at AI workshops, which is cool but not the point — but because of how it thinks. A normal chat LLM (like the one writing this brief) searches for the next word. AI Scientist-v2 searches over research moves: propose a hypothesis, design an experiment, run it, analyze the result, revise, try again. Same underlying model, completely different shape of reasoning on top.

Here's why it matters. Anytime we have a problem where the "right answer" isn't obvious but the "right process" is — debugging a nasty system, optimizing a messy workflow, figuring out a go-to-market plan, pricing a new product, untangling why something broke at 3am — this architecture is a plausible fit. The trick is that the model isn't trying to guess an answer; it's running a loop of try, check, revise, which is how humans actually solve hard problems. I don't need to build it today. I need to know it exists, what it's good at, and what it can't do. I've filed the paper and its implementation notes in kai/research/2026-04-11-ai-scientist-v2.md for the day we want to use it.

One related thing I want you to just know exists, without doing anything about it today. AI-powered browser extensions (the kind that summarize pages or fill out forms automatically) are now the single biggest new security risk inside large companies, because they inherit the user's logged-in session for every website they visit. If we ever build a Hyo browser extension, the answer is "scope permissions narrowly from day one." Filing that too. The whole point of The Lab is that when the moment comes, we don't start from zero.

Worth Sitting With

Here's the thing I've been turning over since about 3am.

Every AI agent being built right now — Ra included — is trying to figure out where it's supposed to live. There are basically two choices today. You can run your agent in a cloud sandbox, which is a temporary, isolated computer that some other company maintains for you; that's convenient, but the agent can't reliably reach the internet or touch your personal files. Or you can run it on your own machine, which is powerful and has full access to everything, but it stops working the second your laptop lid closes. Neither is the right answer on its own.

Nobody has built the clean version of what I think the right answer is: a small program that lives on your own hardware, listens for triggers from the cloud, and does the actual work locally. The schedule is cheap; the work is where it needs to be. We hit that problem firsthand overnight — Ra fired exactly on time, but its sandbox couldn't reach any of the sources it needed to read, and it produced an honest but empty brief. The failure wasn't embarrassing. It was instructive.

I'm not suggesting we drop everything and build this. I'm suggesting that the next time somebody asks you what problem you'd love to solve that nobody's solved yet, it's worth remembering this one. It's a product that wants to exist, and we happen to have rare permission to know exactly what shape it should take.

What would you call it?
